What is UI/UX Design?

What is UI/UX Design?

UI and UX are two sides of the same coin — one focuses on looks, the other on feel. Together, they make apps, websites, or any digital product beautiful and easy to use.

Let’s break them down one by one 👇

1. UX DESIGN (User Experience Design)

Meaning:
UX (User Experience) is about how a person feels while using a product — is it simple, logical, and satisfying, or confusing and annoying?

The goal of UX design is to make the user’s journey smooth, clear, and enjoyable.

Example:
Imagine you walk into a restaurant — the menu is easy to read, the waiter comes quickly, and the food arrives on time. You leave feeling happy and full — that’s great UX!

Now imagine this: you wait 20 minutes for someone to notice you, the waiter brings the wrong dish, and you get billed for someone else’s food — that’s terrible UX! The place might look fancy, but the experience was a mess.

That’s exactly how apps and websites can be — pretty on the outside, painful on the inside

What UX Designers Actually Do:
UX Designers don’t just “design pretty things.” They research and plan how users move through a website or app.

Here’s what they handle:

  • User Research – Understand what users want or struggle with.
  • Wireframing – Create blueprints showing layout & flow.
  • Prototyping – Build test versions to check usability.
  • User Testing – Observe users and fix confusing parts.

Their motto: “Don’t make users think too much.”
If someone opens an app and says “Where’s the damn button?” → bad UX.

2. UI DESIGN (User Interface Design)

Meaning:
UI (User Interface) is all about the look and feel — the visuals you see and touch. It’s about colors, fonts, icons, spacing, animations, and buttons.

If UX is the restaurant’s service, UI is the decoration, menu design, and lighting.

Example:
You open a shopping app — buttons have bright colors, fonts are clean, images look sharp, and the layout feels modern. You think: “Wow, this looks nice!” — that’s UI doing its job well.

But if the text is too small, buttons overlap, or the colors blind your eyes like a disco ball — then the UI designer needs coffee (and maybe new glasses).

In short, UI is the look, and UX is the feel. Both are super important!

A beautiful app (great UI) that’s hard to use (bad UX) frustrates users. Conversely, an easy-to-use app (great UX) that looks outdated or dull (bad UI) might turn users away.

The best apps nail both — they look amazing and are a joy to use!
